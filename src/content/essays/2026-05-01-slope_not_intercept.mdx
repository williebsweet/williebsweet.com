A common piece of hiring advice: hire for slope, not intercept.

Intercept is where someone is today. Slope is the rate they're climbing.

Tempting as it is to optimize for the polished candidate at the top of the y-axis, what you actually want is the trajectory. The person whose curve, projected forward, blows past the one who looks great on day one.

This cuts both ways. Candidates should optimize for their own slope too.

Interview prep is mostly an intercept exercise - you polish the snapshot. The harder work, and the higher-leverage one, happens earlier. Take a holistic look at your career and your interests, find where they meld, and compound on that intersection until you have something only you could have built.

The catch: slope is invisible to most employers. They optimize for intercept because it's legible. Finding the ones who can see and value slope is hard, and partly out of your control.

Luck favors a prepared mind. You can do the work to put yourself in position to create your own luck.

The best guide I've found to building slope is Bill Gurley's [Runnin' Down a Dream](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xmYekD6-PZ8) - originally a talk, [now a book](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/769256/runnin-down-a-dream-by-bill-gurley/). The throughline is compounding focus - pulling ideas from disparate places, investing in them over time, and letting the compounding do the work.

Intercept gets you the offer. Slope gets you the career.
